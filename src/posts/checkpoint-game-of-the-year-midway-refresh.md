We are officially halfway through the year, and the release calendar has already given us plenty to argue about. Instead of standard corporate review scores or generic industry buzzwords, these are the games that are actually holding down my attention as mid-year game of the year contenders. Here is exactly where the race stands, based on what makes each game truly stand out.

The Frontrunner: Resident Evil 9

If I have to pick a definitive favorite so far, it is Resident Evil 9. Capcom is on an absolute tear right now. What makes this one stand alone is how perfectly it breaks its gameplay into two completely distinct vibes by switching between its core characters.

Grace Ashcroft brings back the absolute pinnacle of pure, exhausting survival horror. Her chapters force you into a first-person perspective where you are completely vulnerable, managing tight resources, and genuinely feeling the dread of trying to survive. It is slow, methodical, and incredibly intense.

But just when the anxiety peaks, the game lets you loose as Leon S. Kennedy. Seeing him step back into the spotlight as an older, battle-hardened agent completely carries the narrative weight. His third-person sections function as an incredibly satisfying release valve, letting you utilize his tactical upgrades and physical capabilities to dismantle threats like a total powerhouse. The contrast between Grace's pure terror and Leon's controlled execution is masterclass game design.

The Contenders: Pragmata and Pokopia

Pragmata has been a massive standout because it delivers one of the most unexpected feel-good stories of the year amidst all its sci-fi chaos. At its core, the game focuses entirely on the developing bond between spacefarer Hugh Williams and a child-like android girl named Diana. The writing completely avoids the standard, tired tropes of friction and emotional distance. Instead, watching Hugh naturally step into a protective, nurturing role while Diana reacts to a crumbling lunar station with innocent wonder gives the journey an immense amount of heart. It pairs that emotional anchor with gravity-defying mechanics that force you to rethink how you fight, making it a stellar example of an original IP taking massive creative risks.

On the complete opposite side of the spectrum, Pokopia has earned its spot in the conversation by mastering pure simplicity. It is an entirely different vibe, but it succeeds because it knows exactly what it wants to be and delivers a flawless, incredibly addictive gameplay loop without burying the player under unnecessary menus, microtransactions, or open-world fluff.

The Honorable Mentions

Tomodachi Life gets a serious honorable mention here. It is just pure, unadulterated entertainment. It is the exact kind of pick-up-and-play experience that reminds you why you fell in love with gaming in the first place, punching way above its weight class in terms of personality.

Then there is Forza Horizon 6. Visually and mechanically, it is a masterpiece on wheels because the driving feels spectacular. However, it gets held back from the top tier because it front-loads all its magic. Once you clear out the initial rush of major milestones and max out the main checklists, the staying power hits a bit of a wall. It is an incredible ride, but it runs out of steam faster than the other contenders.

Looking Toward the Horizon

The craziest part of the year is that the board could completely reset before December because the most massive wild cards are still waiting in the wings. Grand Theft Auto 6 is looming large over the entire industry, and the highly anticipated return to Ocarina of Time is still out there waiting to reshape the conversation. Competing alongside them is Marvel's Wolverine, another major wild card that has the potential to completely disrupt the race if Insomniac delivers the gritty, visceral experience they have been teasing.

The first six months set an incredibly high bar, but the race is nowhere near finished.
